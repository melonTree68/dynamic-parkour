## Midterm Submission Requirements

As the final project progresses, each team must submit a **midterm progress report**. The goal of the midterm submission is to demonstrate that your project has moved beyond the proposal stage and that you have made concrete technical progress toward your final goal.

Your midterm report should be written as a **mini paper** that clearly explains your updated problem formulation, technical approach, implementation progress, preliminary experiments, current results, and remaining plan.

### Format Requirements

Your submission must follow these formatting rules:

- The report must be written in **LaTeX** using the **RSS paper template**:
- The report must be **at least one page** and **no more than four pages**.
- References may be included at the end and should be properly formatted.
- The submission should be written in a clear, technical, and paper-like style.

### Required Structure

Your midterm report should be organized as a mini paper and include the following sections.

**1. Project Title.** Provide a concise and descriptive title for your project. The title may be updated from your proposal if your project direction has changed.

**2. Overview or Abstract.** Summarize the project goal, the robot learning or robotics problem you are addressing, your chosen project type, your current technical approach, and the progress made so far.

**3. Problem Statement and Motivation.** Restate the problem you are solving and explain why it is meaningful. If your project scope has changed since the proposal, clearly describe the change and justify it.

**4. Related Work.** Briefly review the most relevant prior work, including your baseline method if applicable. You should explain how your project relates to existing methods and what limitation, extension, or application your work focuses on.

**5. Technical Approach.** Describe your current method in technical detail. This section should include the core algorithm, model architecture, system pipeline, training setup, mathematical formulation, or implementation design as appropriate for your project.

You are encouraged to include diagrams, equations, pseudocode, or system illustrations when helpful.

**6. Preliminary Experiments and Results.** Your midterm submission must include **at least one figure, table, or curve showing experimental progress or preliminary results**.

Examples include:

- Training or evaluation curves
- Success rate tables
- Comparison with a baseline
- Simulation screenshots
- Robot rollout snapshots
- Navigation trajectories
- Manipulation success examples
- Ablation or diagnostic results
- Dataset or system statistics

The result does not need to be final, but it should demonstrate concrete progress. If your current result is negative or incomplete, explain what was learned and how it affects the next steps.

**7. References.** List all cited papers, datasets, software tools, and other resources. References should be properly formatted.

## Academic Integrity

Your midterm submission must represent your own work. If you build on an existing paper, codebase, dataset, or algorithm, you must clearly cite it and explain your own contribution. Direct copying without attribution or superficial modification of existing work will be treated as plagiarism and may result in failure of the project.

## Evaluation Focus

The midterm submission will be evaluated based on:

- Clarity of the problem formulation
- Technical soundness of the proposed method
- Concrete implementation progress
- Quality and relevance of preliminary experiments
- Meaningful use of figures, tables, or result curves
- Feasibility of the remaining plan
- Originality and ownership of the work

The midterm report is not expected to contain final results, but it should demonstrate that the project is technically active, feasible, and on track for completion.

Based on the quality, originality, technical depth, and progress of the submitted projects, we will select several outstanding works as **oral papers**. The remaining projects will be presented as **poster papers**. Teams selected as oral papers will give an in-class presentation during the **last course session**. Poster-paper teams will present their work in poster format, where they should be prepared to explain their project, demonstrate results, and answer questions from course staff and classmates.
